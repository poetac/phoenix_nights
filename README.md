# Phoenix Nights

**The desert still cools off at night. The city doesn't.**

A public-facing app that shows the *true* local climate trajectory ‚Äî especially the divergence of **overnight low temperatures** from their historical norms ‚Äî using official NOAA station data.

> Picking up this project in a new session? Start with [`HANDOFF.md`](HANDOFF.md) (current state, in-flight PRs, environment requirements) and [`CLAUDE.md`](CLAUDE.md) (commands + conventions).

## Why this exists

Mainstream weather apps grade each day against a "normal range" and spotlight the afternoon high. That framing hides the real story in heat-island cities:

1. **Rolling "normals" absorb the warming.** A 30-year rolling baseline quietly bakes 50 years of warming into what counts as "normal." We use a fixed 1970s baseline instead.
2. **The biggest signal is in the lows, not the highs.** Asphalt and concrete release stored heat after sunset; nights warm far faster than days.
3. **Warm nights matter most.** Daily mean = (high + low) √∑ 2, so cooling degree days ‚Äî the standard AC-demand proxy ‚Äî rise just as much from a +6¬∞F shift in lows as from +6¬∞F in highs. Warm nights are also the dominant heat-mortality driver: no overnight recovery for bodies, buildings, or grids.

## The science

The hypothesis ‚Äî Phoenix lows deviating from historic norms faster than highs ‚Äî is confirmed by published research:

- Phoenix annual average low: ~59¬∞F in the 1970s ‚Üí ~65¬∞F by the 2010s (‚âà +6¬∞F). ([Climate Change Dispatch](https://climatechangedispatch.com/news-outlet-blames-phoenix-warming-trend-on-climate-change-leaves-out-uhi-effect/))
- Sky Harbor urbanization raised nighttime minimums ~5¬∞C (9¬∞F) vs. only 3.1¬∞C for daily averages. (Brazel et al., ["Urbanization and warming of Phoenix"](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/226592731))
- Phoenix summer nights warmed ~6¬∞F since 1970 vs. ~2.5¬∞F national average. (Climate Central via [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2023/07/14/1187646149/))
- Urban heat island magnitude: 10‚Äì14¬∞F gap between Sky Harbor and rural stations like Wickenburg, Queen Creek, Casa Grande, and Maricopa. ([ASU Arizona Climate](https://globalfutures.asu.edu/azclimate/urban-heat-island/))
- City of Phoenix: Sky Harbor warming ‚âà 7.5¬∞F, ~3√ó the increase at a nearby rural monument station; nighttime up ~9¬∞F. (City of Phoenix records)

Also wired in:

- **Maricopa County heat surveillance** ‚Äî confirmed heat-related deaths 2015‚Äì2025, hand-verified against the county's [2025 annual report](https://www.maricopa.gov/1858/Heat-Surveillance) (April 2026) and stored with citation in `apps/web/public/data/phx-heat-deaths.json`. It's the only dataset not regenerated by a pipeline; the yearly hand-verification process is written down in [`analysis/HEAT_DEATHS.md`](analysis/HEAT_DEATHS.md). The same file carries a `demographics` block (2024 final report) powering the human-cost card's ‚Äúwho the heat finds‚Äù breakdown ‚Äî homelessness, indoor/outdoor, A/C not working, age, sex, substance use ‚Äî each figure cited with its denominator and framed as a systems failure.
- **US Census decennial counts** ‚Äî Maricopa County population 1950‚Äì2020, used for the population-vs-night-gap card (in `lib/cities.js`).
- **ACIS daily highs** ‚Äî every daily max since 1896 (one ~1 MB request) feeds `analysis/build_heat_season.py` ‚Üí the 100¬∞F-season card.
- **ACIS daily lows + highs** ‚Äî `analysis/build_streaks.py` ‚Üí per-year streak/threshold series (longest run of 80¬∞F+ nights, 110¬∞F+ days, frost nights, cool nights) plus the warm-night *season* span (day-of-year of the first and last 80¬∞F+ night). Validation: 2023's nationally reported 31-day run of 110¬∞F+ days falls out of the pipeline exactly.
- **EIA-930 hourly grid demand** ‚Äî `analysis/build_grid.py` takes `--city` (default `phx`) and fetches July hourly demand for that city's balancing authorities (Phoenix = AZPS + SRP, Tucson = TEPC) from `api.eia.gov` v2 (set the `EIA_API_KEY` env var ‚Äî free key, never committed), emitting July demand-by-local-hour curves per year. The public API serves hourly data from 2019.
- **ACIS 1970s seasonal normals + last night** ‚Äî `analysis/build_normals.py` averages 1970‚Äì1979 daily lows/highs into a smoothed day-of-year baseline (`apps/web/public/data/phx-normals.json`, keyed `MM-DD`, ¬±7-day window). The page then fetches the most recently reported night live (a 2-week ACIS window; Phoenix is UTC-7 so the station's local date is computed without DST) and shows it as an anomaly against that fixed *seasonal* normal in the hero ‚Äî a June night judged against a 1970s June night, not the yearly average.
- **ACIS daily CDD decomposition** ‚Äî `analysis/build_cdd_split.py` splits each year's cooling degree days into a day half and a night half by the exact identity `mean‚àí65 = (Tmax‚àí65)/2 + (Tmin‚àí65)/2` over cooling days (`phx-cdd-split.json`), powering the "thermostat that never turns off" card: because lows outpace highs, the night's share of the cooling bill keeps rising.

Tested and rejected: JJA dew-point trends from the hourly archive show no clean signal at decade resolution (monsoon variability dominates; 1950s mean 54.8¬∞F vs 2020s 50.0¬∞F with non-monotonic decades between) ‚Äî the "drying city" story is not supportable from this station's record, so there is no card. The dew curves remain in `phx-diurnal.json` for future work.

## Methodology

- **Trends:** ordinary least-squares on yearly means, reported as ¬∞F/decade, computed separately for lows and highs over a selectable window (default since 1970). Headline = the ratio (lows warming N√ó faster than highs). The ¬± is a 95% interval from a **moving-block bootstrap** (residuals resampled in multi-year blocks, deterministic so it doesn't jitter), which runs wider than a textbook OLS standard error because consecutive years aren't independent; it's stated as a floor on the uncertainty, not a full accounting of climate autocorrelation. An in-app methodology section spells this out alongside the baseline rationale, station-continuity notes, and the combined-effects (climate + UHI + land use + measurement) attribution statement.
- **Baseline:** fixed 1970‚Äì1979 station average for anomaly views, explicitly contrasted with rolling 30-year "normals."
- **Felt metrics:** nights/year with min ‚â• 80¬∞F; annual cooling degree days.
- **Hygiene:** years missing more than 36 days of observations (ACIS `mcnt`) are excluded, as is the still-incomplete current year (computed dynamically).
- **Data freshness & integrity:** each precomputed asset is stamped with its build date and coverage year (`generated` / `throughYear`); the UI shows "precomputed series current through YYYY" and raises a banner if a static dataset falls behind the live station record. `verify_v0.py` shape-checks every committed JSON (structure, not just values), so a malformed or truncated asset fails CI instead of silently breaking a card. The latest-complete-year cutoff is derived everywhere, never hardcoded.
- **Urban‚Äìrural pair:** Sky Harbor vs. Casa Grande National Monument (`USC00021314`, the same rural control the City of Phoenix analysis used). Over common complete years since 1948, city lows warm ~+1.4¬∞F/decade vs. ~+0.8 in the open desert ‚Äî i.e. roughly half the city's night-warming is the city itself. The decade-average gap grew from ~6.6¬∞F (1950s) to ~10.5¬∞F (1990s), then narrowed slightly as the Casa Grande‚ÄìMaricopa corridor urbanized. Since 1970 alone the city excess is small (~+0.2¬∞F/decade) ‚Äî the card states this nuance rather than hiding it. Reproduce with `python3 analysis/uhi_pair.py`.

## Repo layout

```
apps/web/src/
  CityDashboard.jsx   the page ‚Äî takes a city config, renders the cards
  cards/              one component per card (UHI pair, goalposts, seasons, diurnal)
  lib/cities.js       city registry: station ids, baselines, rural pair, assets
  lib/data.js         ACIS/NCEI/Open-Meteo fetchers, parameterized by city config
  lib/stats.js        OLS with confidence intervals, helpers
  ui.jsx              shared design tokens + Card/Tooltip primitives
apps/web/public/data/ precomputed assets (e.g. phx-diurnal.json)
analysis/             Python verification + precompute pipelines (stdlib only)
.github/workflows/    CI: build + data verification; Pages deploy; scheduled asset rebuild
```

The precomputed assets under `apps/web/public/data/` are committed and served as-is, so they go stale once a new year completes. `.github/workflows/rebuild-data.yml` re-runs the `build_*.py` pipelines (NOAA/EIA are reachable from Actions, unlike some sandboxes), verifies the headline numbers, and commits any refreshed JSON ‚Äî on a monthly schedule, or on demand via *Run workflow*. Run it after shipping a pipeline change to populate new fields.

**Adding a city**: add an entry to `lib/cities.js` (ThreadEx sid via ACIS StnMeta, a rural-pair station, baseline decade, citations) and it joins the `CITIES` registry that the in-app picker (`App.jsx`) iterates ‚Äî the cards, fetchers, and dashboard don't change. The Python pipelines are city-aware too: `analysis/cities.py` mirrors the registry and every ACIS builder takes `--city <key>` (default `phx`, so Phoenix output is unchanged), writing `<prefix>-<asset>.json`. **Tucson** is the second city (`TUSthr 9` "Tucson Area", desert pair Sasabe `USC00027619`); its `tus-*.json` assets are generated by the rebuild workflow (which now loops both cities for the ACIS builders). The grid (EIA-930) and diurnal (NCEI hourly ISD) builders are city-aware too (Tucson = the **TEPC** balancing authority and **Tucson Int'l Airport** ISD); only the heat-death card is Phoenix-only **by design** ‚Äî Pima County's only primary heat-related death series (AZDHS) is ~half undocumented border-crossing desert deaths (a different phenomenon) with a 2023 surveillance-method break and no Maricopa-comparable demographics, so it deliberately isn't reproduced for Tucson (see `analysis/HEAT_DEATHS.md`).

## Sanity checks

Any data pipeline must reproduce these (from the official record):

- 1970s Phoenix annual average low ‚âà 59¬∞F; 2010s ‚âà 65¬∞F.
- TMIN trend since 1970 roughly 2‚Äì3√ó the TMAX trend.
- June normals (1991‚Äì2020, Sky Harbor): high ‚âà 104.2¬∞F, low ‚âà 78.6¬∞F.
- First 90¬∞F+ daily minimum was 1936; the next didn't occur until 1970 ‚Äî now they're routine.
- Even the year's *coldest* night warms: GSOY `EMNT` (annual extreme minimum) trends upward since 1970. (Verified in `analysis/verify_v0.py`; shown by the extremes card.)
- The diurnal range (daily high ‚àí low) shrinks over the Sky Harbor era (1948+): lows rise faster than highs, so the desert's day‚Äìnight swing narrows. (Verified against GSOY; shown by the gap card.)
- The warm-night season is longer than it was in the 1970s: the span from the first to the last 80¬∞F+ night now opens earlier in spring and closes later in fall (`verify_v0.py` re-derives this from ACIS daily lows).
- Second city (Tucson): its overnight-low trend since 1970 outruns both the global background rate and its open-desert pair (Sasabe `USC00027619`) ‚Äî the same city-vs-desert UHI signal Phoenix shows, reproduced live from ACIS in `verify_v0.py`.
- The warm-night season and the 100¬∞F-day season are still longer than the 1970s under *outlier-robust* definitions ‚Äî a 5-of-7-night rule for 80¬∞F nights, and runs of 3+ consecutive days for 100¬∞F days ‚Äî so the lengthening isn't an artifact of a lone freak day at the edges. (Reproduced in `verify_v0.py` from ACIS daily; the cards quote the robust season alongside the headline and lead with the outlier-proof day/night *count*.)
- The live hero's 1970s seasonal-normal low (its fixed yardstick) is ‚âà 80¬∞F in mid-July and ‚âà 40¬∞F in mid-January. (Reproduced in `verify_v0.py` from ACIS 1970‚Äì1979 daily lows.)
- The overnight "cool window" is narrower than it was: hours per typical summer night below 85¬∞F fall from ~6 in the 1970s to ~0 in the 2020s, and the deeper 77¬∞F sleep-recovery window closed after the 1960s. (Derived from the committed diurnal curve; guarded in `verify_v0.py`.)
- The night half of cooling degree days ‚Äî `(low‚àí65)/2` summed over cooling days ‚Äî is a rising share of the annual total versus the 1970s: warmer nights, not hotter afternoons, drive the growth in AC load. (Reproduced in `verify_v0.py` from ACIS daily highs and lows.)
- Both Phoenix's and the open desert's (Casa Grande) overnight-low trends since 1970 outrun the published global background-warming rate (‚âà0.36¬∞F/decade). The global-context card cites that rate as a reference line and labels it a conservative floor (it's an all-hours annual mean; the local figures are night lows). (City and desert trends reproduced in `verify_v0.py` from ACIS.)
